## Highlights

The academic landscape for AI safety on this date is defined by a significant shift towards rigorous, formalized verification of model behaviors under adversarial conditions, alongside a maturation of privacy-preserving computation frameworks that address the limitations of current differential privacy protocols. Three developments stand out as particularly consequential for the field. First, the introduction of **STARE: Step-wise Temporal Alignment and Red-teaming Engine for Multi-modal Toxicity Attack** represents a methodological leap in red-teaming vision-language models. By treating the denoising trajectory of text-to-image generation as an attack surface, this work moves beyond black-box toxicity scoring to expose the precise temporal moments where toxic semantics emerge during synthesis. This white-box approach to multimodal red-teaming provides a granular understanding of vulnerability propagation that was previously inaccessible, fundamentally altering how safety researchers can audit generative pipelines.

Second, the paper **Defense against Poisoning Attacks under Shuffle-DP** addresses a critical gap in privacy-preserving analytics. While shuffle-differential privacy has been touted as a balance between utility and privacy, it historically relied on the assumption of honest user behavior. This work rigorously models adversarial users capable of poisoning attacks within the shuffle protocol, offering a defense mechanism that preserves privacy guarantees even when the honest-user assumption is violated. This is a significant re-evaluation of the security boundaries of distributed privacy protocols, suggesting that current industry deployments may require more robust threat modeling than previously assumed.

Third, the benchmark **ML-Bench&Guard: Policy-Grounded Multilingual Safety Benchmark and Guardrail for Large Language Models** establishes a new standard for cross-linguistic safety evaluation. By constructing benchmarks directly from regulatory texts rather than relying on machine translation or general risk taxonomies, this work ensures that safety guardrails align with region-specific cultural and legal nuances. This addresses a major failure mode in current global LLM deployments where safety policies fail to translate effectively across borders, potentially leading to regulatory non-compliance or cultural insensitivity in high-stakes environments.

## Adversarial Attacks & Robustness

The current day's research output underscores a growing sophistication in adversarial methodologies, particularly within multimodal systems where the attack surface expands beyond text to include visual and temporal dimensions. The work **STARE: Step-wise Temporal Alignment and Red-teaming Engine for Multi-modal Toxicity Attack** challenges the prevailing assumption that image generation is a static black box. By employing a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework, the authors treat the denoising trajectory as the primary attack surface. This allows for a direct white-box analysis of the text-to-image model coupled with a query-only black-box vision-language model. The core innovation lies in coupling a high-level prompt editor with low-level T2I fine-tuning capabilities, enabling the identification of specific steps in the synthesis process where toxic semantics are introduced. This granular visibility is crucial for developing targeted defenses, as it reveals that toxicity is not merely a function of the final output but can be embedded in the intermediate latent states of the generation process.

Complementing this temporal focus, the paper **Jailbreaking Vision-Language Models Through the Visual Modality** explores the underexplored attack surface of the visual component in VLMs. The authors introduce four distinct jailbreak strategies that exploit the vision modality to bypass safety alignment. These include encoding harmful instructions as visual symbol sequences with a decoding legend, replacing harmful objects with benign substitutes to trigger harmful actions, and utilizing visual analogy puzzles that require the model to solve a puzzle to unlock harmful content. These findings contradict the notion that text-based safety filters are sufficient for multimodal models. The visual modality introduces a semantic layer that can circumvent textual guardrails, suggesting that safety mechanisms must be integrated at the embedding level rather than just the token level.

In the realm of data poisoning, **Defense against Poisoning Attacks under Shuffle-DP** provides a necessary countermeasure to the vulnerabilities identified in privacy protocols. The authors demonstrate that existing shuffle-DP protocols are susceptible to poisoning attacks from adversarial users, which can compromise both privacy guarantees and analytical utility. The proposed defense mechanism operates within the shuffle model, ensuring that even when users behave maliciously, the privacy guarantees hold. This work contrasts with earlier assumptions that shuffle-DP was robust against such attacks, highlighting the need for continuous threat modeling in privacy-preserving machine learning.

Further expanding the scope of adversarial analysis, **Repurposing Image Diffusion Models for Adversarial Synthetic Structured Data: A Case Study of Ground Truth Drift** investigates the potential for attackers to repurpose public image diffusion models for generating synthetic tabular data. By reshaping rows of the UCI Adult Income dataset into pseudo-images and applying an unmodified Stable Diffusion U-Net, the study tests the architecture's inductive bias toward spatial locality. The authors find that feature placement becomes a design variable, allowing for the generation of adversarial synthetic data that can drift from ground truth distributions. This philosophical distinction between tabular and image data generation is significant, as it suggests that off-the-shelf generative models can be weaponized for data poisoning in domains where tabular data is the norm, even without specialized training.

The paper **Scale-Aware Adversarial Analysis: A Diagnostic for Generative AI in Multiscale Complex Systems** extends adversarial analysis to physical systems governed by continuous multiscale dynamics. Standard Explainable AI architectures, particularly perturbation-based and gradient-saliency methods, rely on pixel-wise perturbations that generate unphysical artifacts. This work argues that such methods push inputs off the valid empirical manifold, rendering them ineffective for diagnosing generative AI in complex physical systems. The authors propose a diagnostic approach that respects the multiscale nature of the underlying physics, ensuring that adversarial perturbations remain within the bounds of physical validity. This is a significant re-evaluation of how adversarial robustness is measured in scientific AI applications, moving beyond pixel-level metrics to system-level physical consistency.

## Model Alignment & Interpretability

Alignment research continues to evolve from general safety tuning to specialized, domain-specific optimization and interpretability. The paper **Themis: Training Robust Multilingual Code Reward Models for Flexible Multi-Criteria Scoring** addresses the scarcity of research on reward models in code generation. Existing work largely focuses on execution feedback, constraining post-training to optimizing functional correctness over self-contained executable code. Themis-CodeRewardBench is introduced to evaluate multilingual, multi-criteria code reward models. This benchmark allows for the training of reward models that can score code based on multiple criteria beyond mere execution, such as readability, security, and efficiency. This shift enables more nuanced policy alignment in code generation tasks, moving beyond binary pass/fail metrics to a more holistic assessment of code quality.

In the context of red-teaming, **Stable-GFN: Toward Diverse and Robust LLM Red-Teaming via Contrastive Trajectory Balance** proposes a solution to the training instability and mode collapse often associated with Generative Flow Networks (GFNs). The authors eliminate partition function $Z$ estimation in GFN and reduce training instability, which is particularly important in red-teaming where unstable rewards can accelerate mode collapse. By using contrastive trajectory balance, the method ensures that the generated attacks are both effective and diverse. This is crucial for maintaining the integrity of safety evaluations, as a lack of diversity in attacks can lead to false positives in safety assessments.

The paper **Decouple before Integration: Test-time Synthesis of SFT and RLVR Task Vectors** addresses the challenge of integrating Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning from Verifiable Rewards (RLVR). SFT expands knowledge breadth while RLVR enhances reasoning depth, yet integrating these strengths remains a formidable challenge. Sequential training can cause catastrophic forgetting, and joint optimization often suffers from severe gradient conflicts. The authors analyze SFT and RLVR through the lens of task vectors and reveal three structural properties behind these failures: a 30* magnitude disparity, 45* sign interference, and heterogeneous module-wise update dynamics. By decoupling these task vectors before integration, the method allows for the synthesis of complementary strengths without the typical degradation associated with sequential training. This approach offers a pathway to more robust post-training pipelines that can leverage both knowledge and reasoning capabilities effectively.

Regarding interpretability in formal reasoning, **Evaluating the Architectural Reasoning Capabilities of LLM Provers via the Obfuscated Natural Number Game** investigates whether LLM success on formal mathematics benchmarks stems from genuine logical reasoning or semantic pattern matching. The authors identify Architectural Reasoning as the ability to synthesize formal proofs using exclusively local axioms and definitions within an alien math domain. By using the Obfuscated Natural Number Game, which renames identifiers in the Natural Number Game, the benchmark evaluates whether models can adapt to new logical structures without relying on pre-training data. This distinction is critical for assessing the true reasoning capabilities of LLMs in safety-critical domains where generalization to novel logical structures is required.

The paper **Possibilistic Predictive Uncertainty for Deep Learning** addresses the overconfidence of deep neural networks on unseen inputs. Existing methods for uncertainty modeling face a fundamental dilemma: Bayesian approaches provide principled estimates but remain computationally prohibitive, while efficient second-order predictors lack rigorous derivations connecting their specific objectives to epistemic uncertainty quantification. To resolve this dilemma, the authors introduce Dirichlet-approximated possibilistic posterior predictions (DAPPr), a principled framework that balances computational efficiency with rigorous uncertainty quantification. This method allows for reliable epistemic uncertainty modeling without the prohibitive costs of full Bayesian inference, making it applicable for real-time safety monitoring in production systems.

## Privacy Protection & Federated Learning

Privacy-preserving machine learning remains a critical area of research, with new works addressing the limitations of existing protocols in heterogeneous and adversarial environments. The paper **Defense against Poisoning Attacks under Shuffle-DP** highlights the vulnerability of shuffle-differential privacy to adversarial users. While shuffle-DP has attracted significant attention for its favorable balance between privacy and utility, existing protocols rely on the assumption that all users behave honestly. The authors demonstrate that adversarial users can exploit this vulnerability through poisoning attacks, compromising both privacy guarantees and the utility of analytical results. The proposed defense mechanism ensures that privacy guarantees hold even when the honest-user assumption is violated, a significant re-evaluation of the security boundaries of distributed privacy protocols.

In the domain of federated learning, **FedKPer: Tackling Generalization and Personalization in Medical Federated Learning via Knowledge Personalization** addresses the challenge of statistical heterogeneity across healthcare institutions. The global model struggles both to generalize across unseen patient populations and to adapt to the unique data distributions of individual hospitals. This heterogeneity also exacerbates forgetting at both the global and local level. While prior work has largely treated generalization and personalization as separate challenges, this work shows that they must be addressed simultaneously. The proposed knowledge personalization framework allows the global model to generalize while adapting to local distributions without suffering from catastrophic forgetting. This integrated approach is essential for deploying federated learning in medical applications where data privacy and model accuracy are paramount.

The paper **EASE: Federated Multimodal Unlearning via Entanglement-Aware Anchor Closure** tackles the challenge of federated unlearning in multimodal settings. Federated Multimodal Learning trains multimodal models across decentralized clients while keeping their image-text pairs private. However, joint embedding training entangles forgotten knowledge across both modalities and client gradient subspaces, hindering federated unlearning. Previous federated unlearning approaches neither sever the cross-modal reconstruction channel mediated by bilinear coupling nor separate forget-exclusive update directions from those shared with retained clients. The authors identify an Anchor Principle for federated multimodal contrastive unlearning, ensuring that forgotten alignments are properly severed without affecting retained knowledge. This is a critical advancement for privacy compliance in multimodal federated systems.

Further expanding on unlearning, **Unlearning Offline Stochastic Multi-Armed Bandits** initiates the first study of machine unlearning for sequential decision-making systems. Prior work has mainly studied unsupervised or supervised machine unlearning, leaving unlearning for sequential decision-making systems far less understood. The authors formalize the privacy constraint for offline MAB and measure utility by the post-unlearning decision quality. This work provides a foundational framework for privacy-preserving sequential decision-making, ensuring that data deletion requests can be processed without full retraining. This is particularly relevant for applications where privacy regulations require the ability to forget specific data points from decision-making models.

The paper **Zero-Knowledge Model Checking** introduces a technology to formally verify that a software system satisfies a temporal specification of functional correctness without revealing the system itself. The method combines a deductive approach to model checking to obtain a formal certificate of correctness for the system, with zero-knowledge proofs to convince an external verifier that the system complies with its specification of correctness. Proof certificates are represented as ranking functions, and both explicit-state and symbolic schemes are introduced for model checking in zero knowledge. This allows for the verification of proprietary or sensitive systems without exposing their internal logic, a significant advancement for secure software verification in regulated industries.

## Safety Benchmarks & Governance

The development of robust safety benchmarks is essential for evaluating the alignment and security of AI systems across diverse contexts. The paper **ML-Bench&Guard: Policy-Grounded Multilingual Safety Benchmark and Guardrail for Large Language Models** introduces a policy-grounded multilingual safety benchmark covering 14 languages. Existing multilingual benchmarks largely rely on general risk taxonomies and machine translation, which confines guardrail models to predefined categories and hinders their ability to align with region-specific regulations and cultural nuances. ML-Bench is constructed directly from regulatory texts, bridging the gap between general safety and specific legal requirements. This ensures that safety guardrails are not only linguistically accurate but also legally compliant in diverse jurisdictions.

The paper **ControBench: An Interaction-Aware Benchmark for Controversial Discourse Analysis on Social Networks** addresses the challenge of understanding how people argue across ideological divides online. Existing datasets capture only part of this problem: some preserve text but ignore interaction structure, some model structure without rich semantics, and others represent conversations without stable user-level ideological identity. ControBench combines heterogeneous social interaction graphs with rich textual semantics. Built from Reddit discussions on three topics, this benchmark allows for the analysis of controversial discourse in a way that captures both the semantic content and the structural dynamics of online argumentation. This is crucial for studying political polarization, misinformation, and content moderation, providing a more comprehensive approach to understanding the safety implications of social media interactions.

In the realm of prediction markets, **ForesightFlow: An Information Leakage Score Framework for Prediction Markets** introduces an Information Leakage Score (ILS) framework for detecting informed trading on decentralized prediction markets. For an event-resolved binary market, the score quantifies the fraction of the terminal information move priced in before the public news event. Three operational scope conditions are stated as preconditions for interpretation, and the score admits a Murphy-decomposition reading that connects label generation to the proper-scoring-rule literature. A pilot empirical evaluation surfaces findings regarding the detection of informed trading, which is critical for maintaining the integrity of decentralized financial systems. This framework provides a quantitative measure for monitoring information leakage in prediction markets, enhancing the security and fairness of these systems.

The paper **KingsGuard: Enclave Data Protection Under Real-World TEE Vulnerabilities** presents a novel Trusted Execution Environment (TEE) design that systematically monitors and controls the propagation of sensitive data within an enclave. TEEs have emerged as a cornerstone for securing sensitive computations by providing isolated enclaves protected from untrusted software. However, their security guarantees are undermined by vulnerabilities in both the enclave code and the underlying hardware design. KingsGuard enforces fine-grained data flow tracking and checks in hardware, ensuring that sensitive data does not leak despite strong isolation guarantees. This addresses the reality that TEEs are not immune to hardware-level vulnerabilities and requires a more robust approach to data protection within enclaves.

Regarding authentication, **Pick and Sort for Graphical Authentication** proposes a graphical authentication scheme that follows a simple design in which users choose visual elements and arrange them within a grid. Both the number of selected elements and the grid size are configurable, and the visual elements can be customized for specific user groups. A preliminary study with a prototype implementation indicated that the scheme is easy to learn and flexible to deploy. Although login times are longer than those of conventional authentication methods, the additional interaction may be acceptable in scenarios that are not time-critical. This work explores alternative authentication methods that may offer better security against certain types of attacks, such as shoulder surfing or credential stuffing.
